# Motion Graphics Analysis - nateherk, "I asked Claude Code to make me as much money as possible"

Source: 16 contact sheets, 6x6 frames each, sampled every 3 seconds. Timestamps are approximate and inferred from the provided sheet/cell timing rule, so the motion details below describe the visible sampled states plus likely in-between behavior.

## Global House Style

The motion package combines a restrained talking-head edit with high-contrast, game-like interface overlays. The base footage is either a light gray studio talking head or dark screen recording. The animated layer is almost always a teal/green liquid-glass system: translucent black cards, cyan-green borders, soft bloom, inner highlights, rounded corners, thin white labels, and glowing progress fills. Red/magenta is reserved for failure, contradiction, degradation, or warnings.

The recurring motion language is:

- UI elements enter with a small blur-to-sharp resolve, 0.94 to 1.02 scale overshoot, then settle to 1.0.
- Cards slide only a short distance, usually 12-40 px, so the motion feels premium rather than bouncy.
- Glow blooms slightly before or just after the card locks in, creating the feeling that the interface powers on.
- Icons, numbers, and text are staggered inside cards after the glass shell appears.
- Backgrounds use heavy vignette and soft teal light sweeps to separate foreground cards from black negative space.
- Secondary motion matters: borders shimmer, cards float a few pixels, progress bars continue filling after labels settle, and small check dots arrive a beat after the main item.

## Subtle Craft Patterns To Replicate

1. Glow behaves like state, not decoration. Active cards brighten, failed cards tighten into red, completed states get small green dots, and inactive cards stay dim but visible.

2. Most UI entrances are two-stage. First the container arrives, then interior details reveal. This is visible in upgrade cards, income cards, verification cards, and workflow nodes.

3. Stagger timing is short and system-like. The cards usually arrive in a tight cascade, not a long playful bounce. This keeps the tone technical and efficient.

4. Motion is often anchored to the speaker's composition. Chips and lower thirds avoid the face and microphone, using shoulder/chest/upper-corner space.

5. Screen recordings are made cinematic through restraint. The PIP ring, cursor dot, occasional callout frame, and controlled dimming do most of the work.

6. Red is reserved. Because nearly everything is teal/green/white, red warning cards and red word highlights immediately feel meaningful.

7. The background is rarely static black. Teal light waves, vignettes, code texture, or faint orbit rings keep graphic frames alive during holds.

8. Large typography often becomes environment. Giant numbers in agenda cards and scoring charts sit behind readable UI elements, creating depth without adding clutter.
