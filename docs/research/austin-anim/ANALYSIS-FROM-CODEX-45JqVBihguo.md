## Overall Motion Language

This video is Austin's warm, founder-proof variant of the nateherk liquid-glass system. The shared vocabulary is visible throughout: translucent rounded cards, glowing borders, blurred footage as a stage, portrait PIP rails, numbered step cards, document highlights, prompt cards, terminal/browser proof frames, kinetic captions, workflow diagrams, and occasional stat cards.

The extension is the way Austin uses those elements to translate Alex Hormozi's AI process into a teachable operating system. The motion alternates between four evidence modes:

- Document proof: Acquisition.com memos, job postings, Google docs, lists, emails, and chat messages.
- Founder proof: Alex and Leila source clips with lower thirds and warm framed stages.
- Austin proof: overhead paper diagrams, live marker annotations, screen recordings, terminal logs, Claude screens, and Ads Library examples.
- Teaching synthesis: numbered pipeline cards, prompt cards, prompt chaining diagrams, count-up metrics, and blurred thesis captions.

The palette is warmer than nateherk. Nateherk often centers cyan, teal, and cool-green HUD material. Here the teaching graphics are burgundy, rose, magenta, orange, black glass, white text, and occasional purple/yellow/green proof accents. Teal remains mostly in the real studio shelf lights, not in the UI system. A quick still pass can miss that the motion is less about isolated cards and more about repeated proof loops: source clip -> highlighted document -> Austin paper diagram -> copyable prompt -> terminal/product proof -> talking-head synthesis.

Common motion rules:

- Background footage is usually darkened and/or defocused before a card lands. The blur turns the room, whiteboard, or screen recording into a warm stage.
- Glass card bodies enter first with opacity up, blur down, and a small scale settle from about 94-97% to a 101-103% overshoot, then return to 100%.
- Borders and glows bloom after the card body lands. This delayed glow beat is one of the subtle craft signatures.
- Text builds after the shell: title first, then body copy line-by-line, then highlights or active bullets.
- Presenter PIP cards almost always sit in the top z-order, above documents and browser screens. Their rose border is brighter than the main frame border.
- Exits are mostly fast cuts, short dissolves, or blur transitions. Austin spends more design energy on readable entrances and internal emphasis than on ornate exits.
- Analog hand motion does real animation work. Whiteboard/paper lines, marker circles, and underlines are not replaced with vector animations; the live hand becomes the motion layer.

## Subtle Craft Patterns A Still Pass Misses

- The delayed border/glow beat is consistent. Card fill lands first, text resolves second, border glow peaks third. That sequencing makes simple rectangles feel designed.
- PIP always wins z-order. Even when a document or terminal is the main proof, the rose-bordered Austin portrait is visually above it.
- White proof pages alternate with black glass UI. The edit avoids monotony by switching material classes: paper/memo, dark prompt, analog whiteboard, source clip, terminal, browser.
- Red/orange highlights are the reading-order device. Many pages are too dense to read in motion, but the highlight sweep tells the viewer what matters.
- The hand-drawn whiteboard is not a break from the graphic language. It is the analog version of the diagrams, with the PIP and magenta/orange titles binding it to the rest of the system.
- Green appears as execution/progress, not as a brand color. The green progress bar, circles, and "agency killer" card signal "the workflow is running" while magenta remains the brand/teaching color.
- The prompt cards often have a blank or short first state before dense text appears. That tiny staging beat makes them feel generated from the lesson.
- Austin uses editorial blur interstitials as breathers. They prevent card fatigue between long prompt/document sections.
- Source lower thirds are intentionally less glossy than teaching cards. That preserves credibility and differentiates "evidence" from "Austin's synthesis."
- CTA overlays are compact and material-matched. "First link," URL pills, and subscribe cards use the same rounded/glass vocabulary without becoming full ads.

## How This Differs From Or Extends Nateherk

- Warmer palette: nateherk's cool cyan/teal/green liquid-glass look becomes burgundy, rose, magenta, orange, purple, and black glass. Teal is mostly physical room light.
- Analog proof loop: Austin repeatedly uses overhead paper/whiteboard diagrams as a core motion layer. This is a larger departure from nateherk than a simple color swap.
- Founder/documentary evidence: Alex/Leila clips and Acquisition.com memos are not just references; they structure the lesson. The graphic system behaves like a citation machine.
- Prompt pedagogy: copyable prompt cards with PIP are a repeatable lesson module. Nateherk has prompt/code cards, but Austin makes them the main artifact.
- Process-run montage: the green progress strip across terminal/settings/proof frames creates a "workflow executing" feel that is more operational than decorative.
- Native UI tolerance: Austin often leaves Google Docs, Ads Library, terminal windows, and settings screens visibly raw. Nateherk-style polish frames them, but does not over-sanitize them.
- Creator funnel integration: AI Playbook, URL pills, link captions, and subscribe cards are integrated into the same visual material system.

## Implementation Notes For Recreation

- Card entrance: 8-12 frame opacity ramp, blur from 8-14 px to 0 px, scale from 0.95 to 1.02 then settle to 1.0 over 6-10 frames.
- Border/glow: delay 4-8 frames after card body; animate border opacity from 0.2 to 1.0 and outer glow radius/intensity up then down to a stable hold.
- Text reveal: title first, body rows staggered by 4-8 frames, highlights after body is readable. Do not reveal all dense prompt text on the same frame as the card shell.
- PIP: slide from nearest edge 12-32 px, opacity 0 to 1, scale 0.96 to 1.02 to 1.0; keep top z-order; use rose border and soft drop shadow.
- Document highlight: use a left-to-right mask wipe with soft feather; final opacity around 40-60% so source text remains visible.
- Whiteboard: keep real marker motion if possible. If recreating digitally, preserve hand-like anticipation: marker enters, line draws, hand pauses, then label appears.
- Count-up: ease digits with fast-out-slow-in over roughly 12-20 frames; couple the number with a bar fill or pulse so the count feels physical.
- Blur interstitial: increase background blur and dark vignette before text appears; keep text small enough to feel like a thought card, not a headline banner.
- Progress proof montage: keep the green line constant across cuts so unrelated screen captures feel like one process.
