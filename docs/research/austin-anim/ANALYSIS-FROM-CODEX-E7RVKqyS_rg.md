## Overall Motion Language

This video is Austin's warm Claude-code variant of the nateherk liquid-glass system. It keeps the shared grammar: translucent rounded cards, glowing borders, blurred live footage as a stage, persistent presenter PIP, numbered lesson cards, terminal callouts, and over-speaker captions. The differentiator is subject matter and palette. Austin builds the motion system around Claude Code pedagogy: trap labels, permission boundaries, product-doc scaffolds, prompt cards, terminal setup proof, failure diagrams, and example app screens.

The base visual stack is:

- Real camera, source footage, or app UI first gets darkened and defocused.
- A burgundy/orange/magenta "liquid ribbon" or smoky radial glow moves behind the foreground layer.
- A glass card enters with opacity up, blur down, and a small ease-out scale settle, usually from about 94-97% to 101-103%, then back to 100%.
- Border glow blooms after the card body lands. Text often resolves one beat later.
- Austin's PIP sits above nearly every graphic layer, usually with a brighter rose border than the surrounding screen frame.
- Exits are usually editorial cuts, fast defocuses, or the next graphic replacing the current one. The entrance and internal staging carry most of the craft.

Austin's color system is warmer than nateherk's common cyan/teal: near-black glass, burgundy fills, hot magenta edge glows, orange/gold haze, coral Claude pixel mascot, white text, and occasional blue/green only when inherited from UI screenshots. The subtle craft is the repeated "proof plus lesson" alternation: camera explanation, source proof, prompt/terminal proof, diagram, then camera again.

## Repeated Material And Timing Rules

- Card shells arrive before card borders. Austin often lets the translucent fill become visible first, then peaks the rim/glow as a second beat.
- Text enters after the shell, not simultaneously. This is visible in chapter cards, step cards, and prompt panels.
- PIP is almost always last in the z-order stack and often last in the entrance stack.
- Magenta highlights are used for exact action: circled terminal commands, important words, metric circles, active trap labels.
- Orange/burgundy background haze provides depth but rarely competes with white text. It is stronger at the corners and behind ribbons.
- Glass cards use small overshoot, not cartoon bounce. The motion feels quick and polished, with snap coming from cuts and stagger rather than elastic easing.
- Exit design is intentionally simple. Most scenes cut out before a decorative exit can register.

## What Differs From Or Extends Nateherk

- Warmer palette: Austin replaces nateherk's cooler cyan/teal tech glow with burgundy, magenta, orange, and coral.
- Character mark: the coral pixel Claude mascot is a recurring object, not just an icon.
- Prompt-as-artifact cards: long copyable prompts are treated as hero visuals, not secondary captions.
- Terminal safety choreography: exact command/path/permission tokens are circled and taught as motion beats.
- Failure metaphors: the whack-a-mole form diagram and half-baked feature framing are more playful and developer-specific than typical SaaS metric cards.
- Real/generated-output contrast: polished overlays frame intentionally mediocre app outputs, making the critique visual.
- CTA integration: dark product landing-page screens share the same palette, so the promo segment feels like part of the motion system rather than a separate ad break.

## Implementation Notes For Recreation

- Use a 16:9 stage with a dark blurred background layer, then add a slow magenta Bezier/ribbon path behind cards.
- Keep cards at 6-10 px radius, translucent black/burgundy fill, 1 px light rim, outer glow in hot magenta, and a delayed glow pulse.
- Use a quick entrance recipe for cards: opacity 0 to 1, blur 8 px to 0, scale 0.96 to 1.025 to 1.0, over 250-450 ms, ease-out with a short overshoot.
- Use text stagger of 40-90 ms between header, title, and body. Avoid animating every word on prompt cards; make the card readable.
- Use the PIP as a top z-order layer with its own 150-250 ms pop and delayed border glow.
- For command highlights, draw the magenta oval after the terminal is visible, not before. The circle should be tight, imperfect, and short-lived enough to feel like a teacher pointing.
- For blurred caption beats, blur/dim the live footage first, then fade in text. The blur is the container.
