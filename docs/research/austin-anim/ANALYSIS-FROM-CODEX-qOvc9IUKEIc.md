## Overall Motion Language

This video is Austin's warm Claude-Code version of the nateherk liquid-glass system. The base material is dark translucent glass over a blurred burgundy/orange/magenta stage, with white text, hot-pink emphasis, rose borders, and occasional coral Claude/pixel-icon silhouettes. The edit alternates between talking head, source proof, prompt cards, terminal captures, official Anthropic slides, and abstract skill/workflow diagrams.

Compared with nateherk's cooler cyan/teal HUD style, Austin keeps the same premium card physics but shifts the purpose from "game-like metrics and upgrade dashboards" to "educational proof objects." The recurring craft is not just card pop-ins: cards are treated like citation containers. Source clips, prompts, terminal outputs, and documentation screenshots become glass-backed objects that receive highlights, PIP commentary, and guided row scans.

Common animation rules visible across the sheets:

- Glass shells arrive before text. A rounded rectangle, screenshot frame, or slide frame locks in first; labels, highlights, PIP, and arrows follow.
- Entrances are short and soft: opacity up, 10-40 px slide, scale from about 94-98% to a tiny overshoot, then settle.
- Background blur is an animation beat. Austin repeatedly blurs his talking-head layer to create a read zone for overlays, then restores normal footage after the graphic point is made.
- Glows lag the landing by a beat. Borders and magenta blooms brighten after the card is already readable, giving the object a powered-on feel.
- PIP is top z-order. The talking-head inset usually appears after the proof card, with a pink border that keeps Austin present without stealing the first read.
- Highlight motion substitutes for data animation. This video has no obvious metric/stat count-up sequence; instead it uses row highlights, selected code, document highlighter strips, and staged bullet reveals.
- Exits are usually cuts or short dissolves. The visual energy is in entrance, emphasis, guided scan, and hold, not elaborate removal.

## Differences From Nateherk

- Austin replaces nateherk's cyan/teal HUD with warm Claude-coded burgundy, magenta, orange, rose, and coral.
- Austin uses proof-card motion for citations: Anthropic slides, interviews, terminal sessions, documentation, and prompts. Nateherk more often uses app/game dashboards and metrics.
- Austin relies less on stat count-ups and animated progress bars. The dominant "data motion" is row scanning, highlights, selected text, and diagram callouts.
- Austin's blur-over-talking-head staging is stronger. Many graphics appear on top of a defocused live camera rather than on a separate abstract background.
- Austin's PIP is a persistent instructional narrator layer. It is more documentary/educator than stream-overlay.
- Austin uses official source material with minimal restyling. The custom graphics wrap and annotate source slides rather than repainting everything into the house UI.

## Implementation Notes For Remotion Recreation

- Use one shared `WarmGlassCard` primitive: translucent black fill, 1 px gray/rose stroke, 12-18 px radius, inner magenta radial gradient, outer shadow/glow.
- Use entrance timing around 10-16 frames: opacity 0 to 1, translateY 12-28 px or scale 0.96 to 1.01 to 1.0, ease-out cubic/back.
- Delay inner text by 4-8 frames after the shell; delay border glow another 3-6 frames.
- Build `PipFrame` as a top-layer component with fixed pink border and small shadow. Animate it independently after the content object lands.
- Treat screenshot/terminal/source cards as native assets inside the glass system. Do not over-restyle the source; add only border, shadow, PIP, and selective highlight layers.
- For table scans, keep the full table fixed and animate only the active highlight position plus row opacity/brightness.
- For blurred talking-head overlays, animate `filter: blur(0px -> 10px)` and a semi-transparent black gradient mask before revealing text.
- Avoid teal as the primary accent for this creator. Teal belongs to Austin's room lighting, not the motion-graphics system.
